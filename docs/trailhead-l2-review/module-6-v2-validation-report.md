## Summary

Module 6 v1 was a strong structural draft, but it used several product terms that don't exist in — or contradict — the 262 Help portal. These weren't style issues; they were factual claim errors that would have shipped incorrect product names to learners. The v2 draft corrects all four, fixes the structural issues (unit-title/body mismatch, copy-pasted quiz labels, placeholder Resources), strips the one version notation, and grounds every claim.

## Terminology corrections (claim errors)

### 1. "GL Treatment" → General Ledger Account Assignment Rule

**v1 claim:** "GL Treatments are configurations that map billing transactions to the appropriate GL accounts in your COA."
**Finding:** The term "GL Treatment" does not appear anywhere in the 262 Billing snapshot. The object that performs this exact function is the **General Ledger Account Assignment Rule** (also called a general ledger journal entry rule).
**Source:** `ind.billing_general_ledger_account_assignment_rules_and_criteria.htm` — "Define rules to assign accurate general ledger accounts to transaction journals and establish criteria for which these rules must apply to."
**v2 fix:** Renamed throughout — the "How GL Treatments connect" subsection is now "How General Ledger Account Assignment Rules connect," and the lifecycle tables and Key Takeaways use the correct term. Likely root cause: confusion with **Billing Treatment** (a real object in the Billing Policy → Billing Treatment → Billing Treatment Item hierarchy, which is a different layer).

### 2. "Trial Balance View" → General Ledger Accounting Period Summary

**v1 claim:** Unit 2 had a section "Verify Accuracy with Trial Balance Views" describing a "Trial Balance View" with Opening Balance, Debits and Credits, and Closing Balance.
**Finding:** "Trial Balance" does not appear in the 262 Billing snapshot. The record that captures exactly this data — opening balance, total debit, total credit, closing balance, per GL account per legal entity accounting period — is the **General Ledger Accounting Period Summary**.
**Source:** `ind.billing_general_ledger_accounting_period_summary_create.htm` — "General ledger accounting period summaries are created to capture the opening balance and closing balance for general ledger accounts of a legal entity accounting period." Fields: Total Debit Amount, Total Credit Amount, Opening Balance Amount, Closing Balance Amount.
**v2 fix:** Section retitled "Verify Accuracy with General Ledger Accounting Period Summaries"; Unit 2 LO and quiz updated. Added the accurate detail that the closing balance is auto-derived from the GL account's Type, and that summaries are generated by a DPE during period closure (and can be created manually while the period is open).

### 3. Accounting period "types" — removed (fabricated)

**v1 claim:** "Sawyer can configure periods to match how the business runs: Standard Monthly (12 equal calendar months); 15-Day Periods; Custom Calendars (4-4-5 or 5-4-4)."
**Finding:** No such period-type selector exists. Per the snapshot, an accounting period is simply a record with a start date, an end date, and a financial period; it can't exceed one year and can't overlap an existing period. The 4-4-5 references in the 262 snapshot are in *pricing proration* articles, unrelated to accounting periods.
**Source:** `ind.billing_accounting_periods_create.htm` — "Specify a start date and an end date… You can't create accounting periods that are longer than a year, or with start and end dates of an existing accounting period." `ind.billing_accounting_periods_explain.htm` — "a month, quarter, or an year."
**v2 fix:** The fabricated three-type list is replaced with the documented behavior: periods are date-range records (max one year, no overlaps), commonly monthly for internal reporting and consolidated to quarter/half-year/year for external reporting. Added the **Legal Entity Accounting Period** record (links a legal entity to an accounting period), which v1 omitted.

### 4. "Accounting Subledger" → Financial Accounting

**v1 framing:** The module centered on "the Accounting Subledger" as the feature name (~10 uses).
**Finding:** The documented feature name is **Financial Accounting** ("Manage Financial Accounting in Agentforce Revenue Management"). "Subledger" appears in only two snapshot articles, and as a concept, not a feature name.
**Source:** `ind.billing_financial_accounting.htm`.
**v2 fix:** Reframed around "Financial Accounting" as the feature name, while keeping the conceptually accurate point that Salesforce acts as a *subledger* to the ERP's general ledger (Module 1 v2 uses the same "sub-ledger data stays in Salesforce" framing).

### New section — "Other Inputs into Financial Accounting" (foreign exchange gains/losses)

Mike added a Unit 1 section on foreign exchange realized and unrealized gain/loss journal entries. **Grounding outcome: confirmed.**

- **Unrealized gains and losses** — matches `ind.billing_fgn_exch_unrealized_gains_losses_use_case.htm` precisely: at legal entity accounting period closure, a Data Processing Engine job evaluates open foreign-currency balances against period-end rates, creates the unrealized gain/loss dual transaction journals, and creates matching reversal transaction journals dated to the start of the next legal entity accounting period.
- **Realized gains and losses** — the 262 Help portal references foreign-exchange realized gains/losses as a documented topic (`billing_foreign_exchange_realized_gains_and_losses`) and the "Create Transaction Journals for Foreign Exchange Gains or Losses" setting appears in the close and setup articles. Mike's specific mechanics (computed when a payment or credit memo is applied, based on the invoice-date vs. payment-effective-date rate shift) are standard realized-FX accounting and consistent with the documented concept; treated as SME-confirmed.
- Editorial note: the section reads slightly more marketing-toned than the rest of the module ("sophisticated, touchless automation," "smartly pushes," "core competitive advantage"). Substance is accurate; an optional light editorial tightening could bring the voice in line with the surrounding sections.
